# Chapter 3 — How Data and Metadata Drifted Apart

> **Where you are:** Part I — *Why the old model breaks*. Chapter 2 corrected the mental model: data is a product, not inventory. This chapter goes underneath that correction to the mechanism — *why* data and its meaning live apart, why every generation of technology recreated the separation, and why catalogues, the supposed cure, became part of the disease.

---

## The separation nobody chose

Here is a sentence that sounds like an accusation but is not: in most organisations, the data and the knowledge of what the data means are kept in different places, maintained by different people, on different schedules, with no mechanism forcing them to agree. The data lives in databases, object stores, files, and queues. The metadata — the schema, the definitions, the quality rules, the ownership, the lineage, the policy — lives in catalogues, wikis, slide decks, ETL repositories, Confluence pages, and the memory of whoever has been on the team longest.

The two drift apart. They drift because nothing holds them together, and the drift produces a texture of working life that every data professional will recognise: engineers spending afternoons debugging issues that turn out to be a quietly changed definition; governance teams scrambling to patch documentation that was stale the day it shipped; regulators asking questions nobody can answer without a two-week reconstruction project; and the endless, low-grade hum of Slack messages asking *what does this column actually mean?* Everyone agrees metadata matters. Almost nobody makes it intrinsic to how data moves through their systems.

The accusation-that-is-not-an-accusation is this: the separation was never a deliberate decision. Nobody sat in a design review and chose to keep meaning away from data. The separation is an *artifact* — a side effect of how platforms evolved, accreted one generation at a time, each generation solving its own problem and leaving the gap a little wider than it found it. Chapter 1 told that story at the level of eras. This chapter tells it at the level of mechanism, because you cannot fix a drift whose cause you have not located, and the cause is more specific — and more recurring — than "things got complicated."

## A short history of pulling them apart

Trace the separation through the technologies and a pattern emerges that is almost embarrassing in its consistency: every major platform generation got the binding *more* wrong than the one before, and each did so while solving a genuine problem.

**Relational databases got it mostly right.** This is the part the industry has half-forgotten. In a relational database, schemas and constraints live in the system catalog, tightly bound to the tables they describe. The database *cannot* return you a row without also knowing the column types, the keys, the constraints. Data and its structural metadata are, by construction, inseparable inside the system. The relational database is, in this narrow sense, the closest the field ever came to getting binding right — and it got it right by accident of architecture rather than by philosophy. But the binding held only inside the database's walls. The moment you exported data to CSV, Excel, or a flat file — which is to say, the moment data left the building to be shared, integrated, or analysed elsewhere — the context fell away. You were left with numbers and text that could mean anything. The binding was real but local, and the entire history of data integration is the history of data leaving the one place that kept its context attached.

**Hadoop and the early data lakes made it architectural.** Chapter 1 noted this; here is why it matters as mechanism. In the Hadoop world, files lived in HDFS or S3 and their schemas lived in the Hive Metastore or the Glue Catalog — two completely separate systems, connected only by a pointer and a convention. Move a table, change a partitioning scheme, "fix" a layout, and the catalog and the reality silently diverged, with no error, no alarm, and often no way to tell afterward which one was right. The relational database had kept data and schema in one system; the data lake split them into two by design and called it flexibility. The separation was no longer a leak at the boundary. It was the architecture.

**ETL and BI tools made it proprietary.** The third turn of the screw was the tooling layer. Informatica jobs carried their own internal model of what the schemas were — a model that could, and did, diverge from what actually ran in production. Tableau and PowerBI reports embedded their own assumptions about the data, assumptions that could contradict the source database without anyone noticing until a number came out wrong. Each tool built its own private metadata repository, its own fragment of the truth, and none of them was authoritative, because none of them was bound to the data. The meaning of the data was now scattered across as many proprietary stores as you had tools, each confident, each partial, each free to drift from the others and from the data itself.

Read those three movements together and the recurring mechanism is clear. It is not that any one technology was badly designed. It is that **each generation treated metadata as something that lives in a system of its own** — a metastore, a catalog tool, a BI server's internal model — rather than as a property of the data. And anything that lives in a separate system, maintained by a separate process, will drift from the thing it describes, because there is no force binding them. Drift is not a bug in this arrangement. Drift is the *default behaviour* of the arrangement. You would need a mechanism to prevent it, and no generation built one, because for a human consumer the drift was survivable.

## The mess this produced, and why scale changed it

The consequences are familiar enough to list quickly, because every reader has lived them: schema drift and undocumented breaking changes; multiple competing "sources of truth" that all claim authority; governance that depends on documents instead of enforceable rules; and the perpetual Slack archaeology of reconstructing what a column means from people's memories.

At small scale, all of this was tolerable, and it is worth being honest about why, because the honesty is what reveals when it stops being tolerable. It was tolerable because the data estate was small enough that a handful of people held the context in their heads and could be asked. It was tolerable because change was slow enough that documentation could roughly keep pace. And above all it was tolerable because the consumer of the data was a human being with the time and the judgement to read documentation, notice when something looked wrong, and reconcile the competing truths by hand. The drift was a tax, but the tax was paid in human attention, and there was enough human attention to go around.

Scale broke each of those conditions in turn. The estate grew past the point where anyone could hold it in their head. Change accelerated past the point where documentation could keep pace — a carefully built catalogue is 40% out of date within months, not because anyone was negligent but because schemas at scale change faster than humans can document them. And then the consumer changed. Under regulatory scrutiny, with AI models training on the data and AI agents reasoning over it in production, the drift stopped being a tax paid in human attention and became an unaffordable liability, because the human attention that used to absorb it is no longer in the loop. A model does not read the Confluence page. An agent does not remember that this feed is not to be trusted for that purpose. The slack that made the separation survivable for thirty years is exactly the slack that AI removes.

## The cure that became the disease

Faced with this drift, the industry reached for an obvious remedy: build a single place that documents everything. Catalogue the data estate. Define the terms, assign the owners, draw the lineage, publish it all in one searchable portal, and — the theory went — governance would follow. This produced an entire industry of cataloguing tools, glossary workshops, and stewardship programmes, all of them valuable, all of them necessary, and all of them, on their own, increasingly insufficient. It is worth understanding why the obvious cure underdelivered, because the reason is the key that unlocks the rest of this book.

The catalogue's premise is that the primary challenge of metadata is *documentation* — that if you could just describe everything in one place, the problem would be solved. But the primary challenge of metadata is not documentation. It is **latency.** A quarterly-updated catalogue cannot detect a schema change that breaks a regulatory report this afternoon. A manually maintained glossary cannot tell an AI agent, in the moment it forms a response, whether a column contains personal data. A lineage diagram drawn six months ago does not reflect the three pipelines that ran in production last week. The catalogue describes what the data was *supposed* to be, as of the last time someone updated it. It cannot tell you what is happening *now*.

This is the deeper diagnosis, and it reframes the whole effort. A catalogue separated from the data it describes is just one more system holding a fragment of the truth — the most comprehensive fragment, perhaps, the best-organised fragment, but a fragment that drifts from reality exactly like every metastore and BI model before it. Building a better catalogue to fix metadata drift is building a better map to fix the fact that the territory keeps moving. The map gets out of date at the speed the territory changes, and at enterprise scale the territory changes faster than any human cartographer can follow.

So the catalogue, the supposed cure, turns out to be the disease in a more sophisticated form: another separate store of meaning, maintained by hand, drifting from the data, asking humans to keep two things in sync that nothing forces into agreement. The cure shares the disease's fundamental architecture — meaning kept apart from data — and therefore inherits the disease's fundamental failure mode.

## What the diagnosis demands

If the problem is that metadata lives in a separate system and drifts, then no separate system — however well-built — is the answer, because a separate system *is* the problem. The diagnosis points, almost forcibly, in two directions, and these two directions are the spine of everything that follows in this book.

The first is **binding.** If metadata that lives apart from data drifts from it, then metadata must stop living apart. It has to become a property of the data — created with it, versioned with it, deployed with it, travelling with it — so that data and meaning cannot change independently without the change being detected. This is the move from "data plus a catalogue that describes it" to "data that carries its own context." It is the Data Capsule of Part II, and the reason Part II opens the constructive half of the book is that binding is the first thing the diagnosis demands. You cannot run a platform on documentation that drifts; you can run one on context that is bound.

The second is **activity.** Even bound metadata is not enough if it sits there passively, because the failure was never only that the catalogue was wrong — it was that the catalogue was *quiet*. It described; it did not act. The diagnosis demands metadata that does more than document the estate; it must help *operate* it — detect a schema change and halt the pipeline before corrupt data spreads, classify a new field as personal data and apply the policy before a steward meets, tell an AI agent whether a data product is certified before the agent answers. Metadata has to move from the documentation layer to the control plane. This is the move from the passive catalogue to *active metadata*, and it is the second move of the discipline, the subject of Part III. Active metadata does not remove governance responsibility; it makes responsibility *executable* — the difference between a policy written in a slide and a policy enforced by the platform.

Notice that these two directions are not alternatives; they are sequential, and they answer the two halves of the drift problem precisely. Binding stops data and meaning from being able to drift apart in the first place. Activity ensures that when something does change, the system responds at machine speed rather than waiting for a human to notice and a steward to update a description next quarter. Bind, then automate. The structure of the cure follows the structure of the disease.

## Where this leaves us

The separation of data from its meaning was not a mistake anyone made. It was the accumulated default of every platform generation, each of which treated metadata as a thing that lives in a system of its own, and each of which therefore guaranteed that meaning would drift from data because nothing held them together. For three decades, human judgement absorbed the drift, and the catalogue — the industry's best attempt at a remedy — failed not because it was poorly built but because it shared the disease's architecture: one more separate store of meaning, maintained by hand, out of date at the speed of change.

That is the mechanism. It is more specific than "data is hard," and it is more hopeful than it sounds, because a mechanism this clear implies its own remedy: bind the context to the data, and make that context active. Those two moves are the constructive heart of this book.

But before we build them, there is one more thing Part I has to do, and it is the thing that turns this diagnosis from an interesting argument into an urgent one. Everything in these three chapters has been true for years; teams have lived with the lake, the drift, and the stale catalogue for a long time and survived. What changed? What made a chronic, survivable condition into an acute, project-killing one? The answer is the consumer — the arrival of AI, which reads at machine speed, carries no institutional memory, and turns every undocumented assumption into a confident error. The next chapter confronts that failure mode directly, and then draws the map the rest of the book follows out of it.

> **Chapter summary.** Data and metadata drift apart not by anyone's choice but as an artifact of how platforms evolved: relational databases bound schema to data but only *inside* their walls; Hadoop split files from schemas into separate systems by design; ETL and BI tools each built private, drifting metadata stores. The recurring mechanism is treating metadata as something that lives in a *system of its own* — which guarantees drift, because nothing binds it to the data. Human judgement absorbed the drift until scale and AI removed the slack. The catalogue, the obvious cure, failed because it shares the disease's architecture — a separate store of meaning, maintained by hand, defeated by *latency*. The diagnosis demands two moves: **bind** context to data (Part II) and make that context **active** (Part III).

> **Try this.** For one important table, list every place its "metadata" currently lives — the catalogue entry, the dbt model, the wiki page, the glossary, the BI tool's model, the tribal knowledge. Count the stores. Each one is a place the truth can drift, and the count is your drift surface for a single table. Now multiply by the number of tables that matter.
