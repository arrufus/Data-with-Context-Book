# Chapter 21 — Why This Needs a New Operating Model

> **Where you are:** Part V — *Prove*. The previous chapters built the technical case for evidence and certification. This one confronts the uncomfortable truth underneath all of it: the discipline fails without an operating model that funds and empowers ownership. Technology is the easy half. This is the hard half.

---

## The failure that no capsule prevents

Every disaster in Chapter 20 shared a root that was not technical. The churn model drifted because *nobody with authority approved the upstream change* — not because the lineage tool was missing. Maya's model was paused because *the privacy policy and the data answered to different teams* — not because the classification engine failed. Run the whole book's machinery in an organisation where authority is diffuse, consumers are invisible, and ownership is a name in a wiki field, and you will still lose the churn model, because the machinery makes the failure *visible* while the operating model decides whether anyone *answers* for it.

This is the chapter the rest of the book has been deferring, and it can be deferred no longer, because it is the single most common reason data initiatives fail — more common than any tooling gap. The evidence discipline, the certification model, the context data products, the capsules: all of them assume a named owner with a budget, a remit, and the authority to say *wait, there is a model three hops downstream.* Most organisations do not have that owner. They have assignment without authority, architecture without operating model. And two of the most fashionable ideas in modern data — the data mesh and the platform team — both fail in exactly this way, which makes them the perfect lens for the argument.

## Data mesh: an operating model wearing architectural clothes

Five years after the term entered the vocabulary, most organisations claiming a data mesh do not, in any meaningful sense, have one. The most-cited figure — that only 18% of organisations have the governance maturity to run a mesh — comes from a widely cited Gartner data-and-analytics governance survey, which counts organisations whose governance is "mature and scaling across the enterprise" (check the current figure against Gartner's latest, as the number moves year to year). The unflattering corollary: the other **82% have not earned the right to a mesh**, which has not stopped a great many from declaring one anyway.

The failure mode is identifiable from a few hundred metres. An organisation invests in a domain-aligned platform. Storage is decentralised across business-unit accounts. A self-service catalogue is stood up. Domain teams take nominal ownership of their pipelines. The architectural artefacts are all present and visible. The *behavioural* ones are missing entirely: domains own infrastructure but not outcomes, quality is still policed by a central team writing tickets, discoverability still depends on a Confluence page no one updates, consumers still raise tickets and producers still triage them. The mesh exists on a slide deck and in a Terraform repository. It does not exist in the operating rhythm of the business.

Thoughtworks — where Zhamak Dehghani originated the data-mesh hypothesis — is unambiguous after six years of implementations: data mesh is an *organisational transformation, not a technical one*, and the greatest obstacles are behavioural, not technological. It calls out a specific anti-pattern that Meridian's peers commit constantly: IT rebadging existing teams as "domains" — the SAP domain, the Salesforce domain — without genuine business ownership, mandate, or decision rights. Re-read the four founding principles — domain ownership, data as a product, self-serve platform, federated computational governance — and it is striking that *three of the four describe organisational behaviour.* Only one is recognisably technical. Yet organisations gravitate to the architecture, because architecture is tractable: it can be procured, contracted, prototyped, demonstrated to a steering committee. Operating models have to be *earned* — boards approve technology programmes but rarely approve multi-year programmes to redistribute decision rights and accountability. So the most demanding principle, federated computational governance, is treated as the one to skip, and it is precisely the principle that determines whether anything holds together at scale.

## Product thinking is the gating capability

The most reliable difference between organisations that make mesh work and those that produce a costly relabelling exercise is whether domains genuinely operate as **product organisations.** "Data as a product" has suffered most from overuse — hollowed out into "a table with a README," or lately "a schema in the catalogue with a lineage diagram." In the original specification it carries real weight: identified consumers, an articulated value proposition, contracts, roadmaps, lifecycle management, deprecation rules, support obligations, measurable usage and trust metrics. None of these are storage or compute concerns. All require *funded, named ownership.*

The role most organisations have not staffed is the **data product manager** — an accountable owner with a roadmap, a backlog, and authority over consumer relationships. Without it, domain ownership collapses back into engineering ownership; engineering ownership collapses back into central platform ownership; and the mesh dissolves into the warehouse it was meant to replace, typically within twelve to eighteen months. This is the deepest reason Meridian's suitability product works: not because the graph technology is good, but because **Maya is a funded data product manager with a real remit** — she can approve a change, block one, name her consumers, and answer for the product's fitness. Strip that role out and every technical layer in this book still stands and still fails, because there is no one with the authority to keep it true.

The contract layer is what makes federated ownership tractable at scale. Without enforceable producer-consumer contracts — which the Open Data Contract Standard and similar specifications now make practical — federation degenerates into chaos or, more commonly, a quiet reassertion of central control: the platform team, exhausted by ad-hoc disputes, becomes the de facto arbiter and the operating model reverts. This is the same contract that Chapter 6 introduced as the capsule's outer envelope and Chapter 15 extended with semantic obligations. It is not a governance nicety; it is the load-bearing member of the operating model.

Two anonymised financial-services patterns make the point concrete. A global universal bank, documented across public conference sessions, owns each data product through a triumvirate — business owner, technical owner, a small team of domain-expert engineers — organised around business constructs (wholesale credit risk, trading data, reference data) rather than source systems, and pointedly did *not* migrate its most regulated data first, recognising the operating model had to mature before the highest-stakes domains could transition. Five years on it continues to expand, because the foundation was product and governance, not storage and compute. That is what the 18% looks like. The inverse — a UK asset manager that reorganised existing central teams into "domains," procured a catalogue, re-tagged the same warehouse tables as "data products," created no product-manager role, and whose consumers reported no perceptible change eighteen months in — is what the 82% looks like. The two differ on essentially one axis: whether product and governance were *treated as the work itself* or as enabling functions to be back-filled once the platform shipped. The platform always ships. The disciplines almost never get back-filled.

Keep this diagnostic. A "mesh" that is really a rebranded warehouse shows four or more of: a central team still owns the substantive ingestion and curation; "domains" map to source systems rather than business capabilities; there is no product backlog distinct from the engineering backlog; quality SLAs are aspirational, not contractual; discoverability depends on a wiki rather than a marketplace with usage telemetry; the catalogue is populated by automation but curated by no one; consumers still raise tickets and producers still triage on best effort. Tellingly, the 18% who succeed rarely talk about "mesh" anymore — they talk about products, contracts, and consumers. The terminology recedes once the operating model takes hold, because the operating model was always the point.

### The target operating model, drawn out

"Funded, named ownership" is a phrase until you draw the structure it implies, so here is Meridian's target — the shape the discipline needs underneath it:

- **Domain product teams** (e.g. Wealth Client, under Maya) own their data products end to end: a *data product manager* (accountable), a *technical lead*, and a small team of domain-expert engineers. This is the triumvirate the successful bank used — business owner, technical owner, engineers — organised around business capabilities, not source systems.
- **A platform team** owns the paved paths, the CI, the active-metadata layer, and the standards-based graph — measured, per the platform-team section, on the domains' outcomes, not its own throughput.
- **A governance function** owns the policy standards, the classification taxonomy, and the certification framework — federated, setting the rules the domains govern *within*, not policing every ticket.

And because the role most organisations have not staffed is the data product manager, here is the job, in brief, as a gift to readers who have to hire one:

> **Data Product Manager.** Accountable owner of one or more data products. Owns the roadmap and backlog; decides schema, contract, and definitional changes; signs off AI use and quality thresholds; manages consumer relationships, deprecation, and support obligations; is measured on consumer trust and adoption, not pipeline throughput. Holds a budget and decision rights — the "A" in the RACI. Not a steward (who keeps documentation) and not an engineer (who builds pipelines): the person who answers for the product's fitness and can say *wait* to an upstream change.

### Funding is the quiet determinant

How the model is *funded* decides whether it survives, and it is the part boards find hardest. Three patterns: **cost-centre** (the data team is overhead, funded annually, products compete with everything else for scraps — the model under which products get deprioritised and ownership decays); **product-funded** (each domain funds its product team as a standing capability with a roadmap — the model that lets Maya plan beyond the current quarter); and **chargeback** (consumers pay for the products they use, which sharpens accountability but can suppress the shared-infrastructure investment). Meridian funds domain products as standing product teams, because *annual project funding kills products* — a product needs an owner with a roadmap that outlives a project's end date, and the moment funding lapses between projects the ownership lapses with it and the mesh dissolves into the warehouse. The single most consequential decision a board makes here is not which platform to buy; it is whether to fund data products as durable capabilities or as projects that end.

## Platform teams: the enabler that becomes the gate

If the data mesh shows ownership collapsing *inward* for lack of a product operating model, platform teams show the same disease from the other direction — centralisation that was meant to enable becoming the thing that delays. Gartner forecasts that by 2026, 80% of large engineering organisations will have platform teams, up from 45% in 2022, and the rationale is sound: one team absorbs the cognitive load — Kubernetes, observability, secrets, identity, network policy, FinOps, compliance — so delivery teams can focus on outcomes. That contract, articulated by the Team Topologies authors, is what justifies the investment: the platform is an *internal product* that reduces cognitive load through a compelling self-service interface.

The drift from enabler to gatekeeper is rarely deliberate; it happens through four predictable failure modes, each a control added with good intentions and never retired. **Ticket-driven workflows**: a five-minute change waits four days for a human, and the signal that the platform has become an internal IT department is that delivery teams start maintaining shadow infrastructure to bypass the queue. **Rigid standards**: a rule written in 2022 to solve a 2022 problem quietly becomes a 2026 obstacle, enforced by reflex because there is no review cadence, and teams learn that justifying a deviation costs more than complying with a rule that no longer makes sense. **Slow, opaque onboarding**: a week to provision an environment, documentation out of date before it is published — the platform's product quality declaring itself, unflatteringly. **Governance creep**: each incident produces a new control, each audit a new requirement, controls accumulate and are never retired; the ratchet only turns one way, until the platform's compliance cost to delivery teams exceeds the complexity it was meant to abstract, and it has inverted its own purpose.

The corrective is the same word that fixes the mesh: **product.** Self-service that means the elimination of human intervention from routine requests, not a form that routes to a human. **Paved paths, not walled gardens** — make the right thing the easy thing without making the wrong thing impossible, with an escape hatch any team can take with reasonable justification and without convening a committee. Internal UX treated as a first-class concern, which means putting a product manager, and a documentation quality bar, on the platform team. And measuring the platform by *its customers' outcomes* — time to first deployment, self-service ratio, escape-hatch usage read as a signal about the standards rather than the teams, internal NPS, and the DORA metrics of the delivery teams it serves — not by its own uptime and ticket-closure counts. A platform with 99.99% uptime and forty onboarded services can still be the single largest drag on enterprise delivery velocity. The failure, again, is not technical; capable engineers build these bottlenecks. The missing ingredient is product discipline — the willingness to treat internal users with the rigour external product teams apply to customers.

## The controls taxonomy, and where certification sits

Both failures — the collapsing mesh and the calcifying platform — resolve through the same distinction about *where controls sit and who owns them.* A workable taxonomy has three tiers, and it is exactly how Meridian should place the evidence and certification machinery of this part. **Non-negotiable controls** imposed by external obligation — DORA, BCBS 239, GDPR, MiFID II, the EU AI Act — should be *encoded into the platform and enforced by automation*, making compliance the default state rather than a human approval gate. This is where certification's automatable checks live: lineage completeness, policy enforcement, contract versioning, enforced at the boundary. **Strongly recommended patterns** — architectural conventions, the paved paths — express an opinion, make the recommended path frictionless, and tolerate well-reasoned deviation. **Team discretion** covers everything else, where the platform has no opinion and adds no overhead. Certification's *human attestations* — is the meaning correct, is this use appropriate — sit with the funded owners and the risk function, not the platform. Put the machine-checkable controls in the automated platform and the judgement calls with named, funded owners, and certification stops being theatre and becomes the operating model expressing itself.

## Federated governance, as decision rights

"Federated computational governance" is the mesh principle most often skipped and least often specified, so specify it: it is a *decision-rights table* separating what is decided globally from what is decided in the domain. Global, or nothing interoperates; local, or the centre becomes the bottleneck the mesh was meant to remove.

| Decision | Global (governance/platform) | Domain-local |
|----------|:---:|:---:|
| Identity / URN scheme | ✓ | |
| Classification taxonomy | ✓ | |
| Contract standard (ODCS) | ✓ | |
| Certification framework | ✓ | |
| Business definitions & rules | | ✓ |
| Quality thresholds per use | | ✓ |
| Product roadmap & deprecation | | ✓ |
| Who consumes the product | | ✓ |

The line is the whole art of federation: the centre owns the *rules of interoperability* (so a Meridian client id means the same everywhere, and every product speaks ODCS), and the domain owns the *meaning and lifecycle of its own products* (so Maya decides what "suitable" means for wealth, not a central committee). Get the line wrong toward the centre and you have recreated the central data team; wrong toward the domains and you have chaos with no interoperability. The table is where "federated computational governance" stops being a slogan and becomes an operating decision.

### Team Topologies, mapped

For readers who think in Team Topologies terms, the mapping is clean and worth stating: domain product teams are **stream-aligned** (aligned to a business capability, owning their products end to end); the platform team is a **platform/enabling team** (reducing the domains' cognitive load through paved paths and self-service); and the governance function is a **complicated-subsystem team** (owning the specialist standards the domains consume). The interaction modes matter: platform-to-domain is *X-as-a-service* (self-service, not tickets), and governance-to-domain is *facilitating* (setting standards and helping domains meet them, not gatekeeping). Naming the topology is not academic; it is what tells you when an interaction has gone wrong — a platform team taking tickets instead of offering self-service has slipped from *X-as-a-service* into *collaboration*, which is the bottleneck drift by another name.

### The transition, and two arcs completed

None of this appears by decree; it is an 18-month transition from centralised to federated, and the two hardest handoffs are best told as the completion of two characters' arcs.

**Tom's handoff: from quality policing to contracts.** Tom's central quality team, over the transition, stops *assessing* every product against a generic standard and starts *enabling* — building the testing harnesses, the quality-as-code templates, the API that lets each domain contract quality with its own consumers. His award-winning programme is not dismantled; it is inverted, from a central assessor to a platform capability the domains use. The team that once owned the composite score now owns the tooling that lets fifty products each prove fitness for their own use. It is a demotion of the score and a promotion of the discipline, and Tom, to his credit, comes to prefer it.

**Helena's handoff: from catalogue curation to source-sync.** Helena's team stops *curating* the catalogue by hand — the losing race against drift — and starts *operating the sync* that keeps the catalogue reflecting the capsule source (Chapter 14's feedback loop). Her £400k catalogue is not written off; it is repositioned from a documentation graveyard into a live index over the source of truth, and her stewards' enrichments now flow back to Git as pull requests rather than dying in a portal. The catalogue that could not answer the AI programme's twenty-four-hour question now can, because it is fed by the source rather than maintained beside it. Both arcs make the same point: the transition does not eliminate the central functions; it *repositions* them from owning-the-work to enabling-the-domains, which is the only version of federation that holds.

### Measuring the operating model itself

Finally, the operating model needs its own instrument panel, distinct from the data metrics: **product NPS** (do consumers value the products?), **contract-violation rate** (is federation holding, or are producers breaking consumers?), **escape-hatch usage** (are the platform's paved paths good enough to choose, or are teams routing around them?), and **time-to-new-product** (is the discipline getting cheaper to apply, or heavier?). These measure whether the *operating model* is working, as opposed to whether any given product is — and a healthy set of these is what tells a board the federation is real and not, once again, a warehouse with new branding.

## What the operating model has to fund

Pull the two failures together and the prescription is one sentence: **the discipline of this book requires funded, named ownership with real authority, and a contract layer that makes that ownership tractable at scale.** Everything else — capsules, metadata as code, active metadata, context products, certification — is the machinery that ownership operates. The machinery without the ownership is the 82%'s rebranded warehouse and the gatekeeping platform team: architecture that shipped, disciplines that were never back-filled, failures that stay visible and unowned.

For regulated firms the calculus is unforgiving in a particular way. The strongest case for federated ownership — BCBS 239 lineage, MiFID II reporting domains, the audit questions of Chapter 20 — exists precisely where governance maturity is high but *centralised by long-established design*. Translating that centralised maturity into federated maturity is where most programmes stall, because it requires the centre to give up control *before* the domains have proven they can hold it. That is a leadership act, not a technology purchase, and it cannot be bought. The good news, such as it is, is that the discipline can be *built* — but only if the organisation stops trying to buy it, and instead funds the product managers, empowers the owners, enforces the contracts, and measures the platform by whether everyone around it moves faster.

The book has now made its complete case: bind context to data, keep it active, package it for reasoning, prove it with evidence — on an operating model that funds the ownership all four moves depend on. One question remains, and it is the one a finance director asks first: is it worth it, and how would we know? That is the subject of the next chapter.

If the answer is a team, a committee, or a ticket queue, no amount of the technology in this book will save the product. Fund the owner first.
