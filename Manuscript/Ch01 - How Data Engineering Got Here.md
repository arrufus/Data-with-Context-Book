# Chapter 1 — How Data Engineering Got Here, and Where It Broke

> **Where you are:** Part I — *Why the old model breaks*. These first four chapters are the on-ramp. They make the case that the foundation most data platforms stand on was built for a consumer — the human analyst — who is no longer the one that matters most. The discipline itself begins in Part II. First, the diagnosis.

---

A settlement mainframe installed in 1994 still clears every trade Meridian makes. Two floors of abstraction above it, a cloud data warehouse from 2013 holds the curated marts the analysts actually trust. Half the firm's domains have migrated to an Iceberg lakehouse; the other half have not, and nobody can say when they will. And reaching across all of it, live since this year, are two AI systems — an adviser copilot answering relationship managers' questions about client portfolios, and a suitability model heading for its first review by the model-risk committee.

Nobody designed this estate. It accreted. Each layer was the right answer to the problem of its decade, laid down on top of the last while the last kept running — so that what Meridian operates today is not an architecture but a geological cross-section: four eras of data engineering stacked, all of them still live, none of them ever quite switched off. This is the estate nearly every enterprise now brings to AI — not a clean foundation but a sediment. To see why AI breaks on it, you have to read the layers. And to read the layers, you have to know what each one was built to do, and what it quietly failed to do.

## One job, four solutions, one problem nobody solved

Strip away fifty years of acronyms and data engineering has only ever had one job: to reliably and efficiently turn raw data into useful information. Every era of the field — the relational database, the data warehouse, Hadoop, the cloud, the lakehouse — was a new answer to that one demand, shaped by whatever constraint was most painful at the time. Storage was scarce, so we structured. Then volume exploded, so we distributed. Then latency mattered, so we streamed. Then everything fragmented, so we tried to unify.

The lineage is impressive, and the platform you inherited is a sediment of it — which is why the history is practical rather than nostalgic. But the useful way to read it is not as a march of progress. It is as a sequence in which **each era solved its predecessor's central problem and, in doing so, created the one its successor would have to solve.** And running underneath all of it, never quite addressed by any era, is a single quiet problem that grew a little larger with each generation: the meaning of the data kept getting separated from the data itself.

This chapter tells that story quickly, because the point is not nostalgia. The point is that the problem none of these eras solved — context, divorced from data — is the exact problem that the arrival of AI has turned from a chronic inconvenience into the binding constraint on whether your data delivers any value at all. To see why the old model breaks, you first have to see what it was built to do, and what it always quietly failed to do.

## The foundations: structure and the single source of truth (1970s–1990s)

The field's roots are in the relational database. Edgar Codd's relational model in the 1970s gave organisations a systematic way to store and retrieve structured data; RDBMS platforms like Oracle followed, and SQL became the lingua franca it remains today. By the 1980s and 1990s, businesses were drowning in operational data spread across systems that could not talk to each other, and the answer was the **data warehouse** — a central repository that aggregated data from many sources into one place fit for analysis. Bill Inmon formalised the warehouse; Ralph Kimball brought dimensional modelling. The **ETL** process — extract from source systems, transform into a consistent shape, load into the warehouse — became the central craft.

The problem this era solved was **integration and consistency**. Before it, every department's truth was its own; after it, an enterprise could have a single source of truth for reporting, and a business intelligence layer on top of it. That was a real and lasting achievement.

But notice what the warehouse also did, almost invisibly. To build it, you modelled the data — you decided what the columns meant, how entities related, what the business rules were — and that modelling work lived in a *separate* place from the operational data it described. The meaning of "active customer" lived in the warehouse's schema and in the heads of the modellers and in the ETL transformation logic. The operational systems that generated the data knew nothing of it. For the first time, at scale, the data and its definition lived in different buildings. In the 1990s this was tolerable, because the people doing the modelling were also the people doing the analysis, and the institutional memory held. The gap was there. It just had not started to hurt yet.

## The big-data era: scale, variety, and the divorce of data from schema (2000s)

Then the web happened, and the constraint changed. The early 2000s brought an explosion in the volume, velocity, and variety of data — the "three Vs" — that monolithic databases simply could not hold. Google's papers on the Google File System (2003) and MapReduce (2004) described how to store and process data across large clusters of cheap machines, and the open-source **Hadoop** project (2006) put that capability in everyone's hands: distributed storage (HDFS) and distributed compute (MapReduce) on commodity hardware. Alongside it came **NoSQL** databases — Cassandra and HBase, both descendants of Google's Bigtable paper — for semi-structured and unstructured data at a scale relational systems could not reach.

The problem this era solved was **scale and diversity**. You could now store and process logs, clickstreams, images, and sensor feeds that the warehouse would have choked on. Data engineers in this period were part software developer, part systems engineer, wrangling MapReduce jobs and Hive scripts and keeping clusters alive.

And here the quiet problem stopped being quiet. In the Hadoop world, the data was files in HDFS, and the schema describing those files lived in a *completely separate system* — the Hive Metastore, or later the AWS Glue Catalog. Move a file, change a partition layout, "fix" a directory structure, and the catalogue and the reality silently diverged. The relational database had at least kept schema and data bound together in the same system; the data lake pulled them apart by design and made the separation architectural. The thing that gave data its meaning — its schema, and beyond that its semantics — was now a different system from the data, maintained by a different process, and free to drift. This is the structural origin of a problem this book will spend its length addressing, and it was born here, as a side effect of solving scale.

## The cloud and real-time era: elasticity, speed, and the named role (2010s)

The 2010s brought two more shifts. First, the cloud. AWS, then Google Cloud and Azure, turned infrastructure into an on-demand utility, removing the need to own and tune clusters and making large-scale data work accessible to small teams. Cloud data warehouses — Redshift, BigQuery, Snowflake — decoupled storage from compute and gave analysts fast SQL over enormous datasets without the administrative burden of the old appliances. Second, real time. Batch Hadoop jobs introduced delays that finance, telecoms, and gaming could not tolerate, and so **Apache Kafka** gave us a distributed log for streaming ingestion, while Spark, Storm, and Flink gave us ways to compute over data in motion. **Apache Airflow**, open-sourced by Airbnb in 2015, made complex pipelines into versioned, scheduled, monitorable code.

The problem this era solved was **elasticity and speed**, and it also, by the mid-2010s, gave the field its name: the "data engineer" became a recognised role, sitting between the raw sources and the data scientists and analysts who needed clean, timely data. The modern data stack was born — a modular assembly of best-of-breed tools, each excellent at one job: Fivetran to ingest, Snowflake to store, dbt to transform, Airflow to orchestrate, a catalogue to document.

But modularity has a cost, and it is the cost this era handed forward. A world of specialised, interoperable components is also a world of seams — and the context of the data now had to survive being passed across all of them. The schema lived in the warehouse; the transformation logic in dbt; the orchestration in Airflow; the documentation in a catalogue; the business definitions in a glossary or a wiki or a slide deck. Each tool held a fragment of the data's meaning, and none held all of it. Commentators began to talk about a "crisis of integration" — the irony that assembling many best-of-breed tools could create as many silos as it dissolved. The meaning of the data was now scattered across a dozen systems, and keeping it coherent was a job nobody owned.

## The modern era: the lakehouse, and the debt coming due (2020s)

Which brings us to now. Modern data engineering is defined by the attempt to unify what the previous eras fragmented. The **lakehouse** — Delta Lake, Apache Iceberg, Apache Hudi — adds a transactional, schema-aware layer on top of cheap object storage, trying to combine the flexibility of the lake with the reliability of the warehouse. The discipline of **DataOps** brought software-engineering rigour — version control, testing, CI/CD — to pipelines. Governance, lineage, and data quality moved from afterthoughts to standing concerns, pushed by regulations like GDPR and HIPAA. And machine learning moved from the lab into everyday operations, making the data engineer the person who feeds the models.

The problem this era is trying to solve is **fragmentation and trust** — the accumulated debt of everything that came before. And it is here, finally, that the quiet problem presents its bill. Three eras of separating data from its meaning — modelling in a separate layer, schema in a separate system, semantics scattered across a dozen tools — have produced data estates that are vast, fast, elastic, and profoundly hard to trust, because no one can reliably say what any given number *means*, where it came from, or whether it is fit for the purpose at hand. We built extraordinary machinery for moving data and almost no machinery for keeping its context attached to it.

For most of this history, that was survivable, because of one thing: the consumer of the data was a human being. A human analyst who saw a strange revenue figure would investigate. A human who encountered a column called `cust_seg_3` would ask someone, or remember, or work it out. Humans carry context in their heads and supply it on demand; they bridge the gap between data and meaning instinctively, every day, without anyone noticing that a gap is being bridged at all. The whole edifice rested, silently, on human judgement filling in what the data could not say for itself.

## The eras still in the room

It is tempting to read that history as a sequence of replacements — the warehouse gave way to Hadoop, Hadoop to the cloud, the cloud to the lakehouse — but that is not how it played out in any real organisation, and certainly not at a firm like Meridian, the wealth manager whose story runs through this book. Nothing was ever fully replaced. The eras *accumulated*, each new layer added on top of the last while the last kept running, so that the platform you actually inherit is not the latest architecture but a geological cross-section of all of them at once. This makes the history practical rather than nostalgic: read as sediment, it becomes an assessment lens you can point at your own estate.

Meridian runs something from every row. A settlement mainframe nobody wants to touch. A decade-old warehouse of curated marts. An Iceberg lakehouse that half the domains have moved to and half have not. And, newest, two AI systems reaching across all of it. When we say the old model breaks, we do not mean an old *system* fails; we mean the *assumption* common to every layer — that context can live somewhere other than the data — finally stops being survivable. The assessment exercise this table invites is the one to do before any AI programme: for each era still running in your estate, ask where that layer keeps the *meaning* of its data, and how far that store has drifted from the data itself. The answer, era by era, is the shape of your context debt.

## A worked genealogy: one metric, four fractures

The abstraction "meaning gets separated from data" is easier to believe when you follow a single number through the eras and watch it fracture. Take the most ordinary metric a business has: the count of *active customers*. At Meridian, read *active clients*.

In the **warehouse era**, "active" was defined once, by a modeller, encoded in an ETL transformation and a schema comment, and it held — because the same small team that defined it also consumed it. One definition, one place, one team. The gap between data and meaning existed (the definition lived in the ETL, not the operational system) but it was bridged by proximity.

In the **big-data era**, the raw events landed in HDFS and the definition of "active" moved into a Hive query, or a Pig script, or a Spark job — one of several, written by different people for different purposes. The events knew nothing of "active"; the meaning lived in whichever job you happened to run. Two teams computing "active" from the same files now got two different numbers, and nothing in the files could adjudicate. **First fracture:** the definition detached from the data and multiplied.

In the **cloud / modern-stack era**, "active client" became a dbt model — governed, versioned, tested, which is genuinely better — but *also* a Looker measure, *also* a filter baked into three dashboards, *also* a WHERE clause in a marketing export, *also* a sentence in a glossary that no longer matched any of them. Each tool held a fragment of the meaning, and the fragments diverged at the seams *between* tools: the seam where ingest hands to transform, where transform hands to BI, where BI hands to reverse-ETL. **Second and third fractures:** the meaning spread across the modular stack and drifted at every handoff.

By the **lakehouse era**, the divergence is so normal that answering "how many active clients did we have last quarter?" honestly requires a reconciliation project — which is exactly the scene Chapter 2 opens with. And when, latterly, an AI copilot is pointed at this estate and asked the same question, it does not reconcile. It picks one fragment — whichever table or measure it can reach — and answers with total confidence, and the answer is one of the several wrong ones. This is the Okonkwo pattern the book returns to in Chapter 5, in miniature: not dirty data, but *fractured meaning*, consumed by something that cannot tell the fractures apart. The genealogy of one metric is the genealogy of the whole problem.

## What didn't happen: three attempts to keep meaning attached

A fair objection at this point is: *surely someone tried to fix this before?* They did. The industry made at least three serious attempts to keep meaning attached to data, and understanding why each stalled pre-empts the reasonable worry that this book is proposing something already tried and failed.

**Master Data Management (MDM)** tried to create a single golden record for core entities — customer, product, instrument — with survivorship rules reconciling conflicting sources. It was, and is, valuable, and Meridian runs it for client identity. But MDM governs a narrow set of *entities*, not the meaning of every metric, feature, and field across the estate; it is a hub the rest of the platform must choose to consult, not a property that travels with the data. It reduced fracture for a few master entities and left the long tail untouched.

**Enterprise Information Integration (EII)** and its data-virtualisation successors tried to present many sources through one virtual layer, so meaning could be defined once at the federation point. But the semantics still lived in the virtualisation server — one more separate system, drifting from the sources beneath it — and the approach struggled with scale and performance. It moved the separate store of meaning; it did not abolish it.

**The semantic web (RDF, OWL, linked data), circa the 2000s** had exactly the right instinct — bind machine-readable meaning to data through shared ontologies — and largely failed to cross into the enterprise mainstream, defeated by tooling immaturity, the cost of authoring ontologies by hand, and the absence of a consuming workload urgent enough to justify the effort. That last point is the one that matters, because it has now changed. The semantic web lacked a consumer that *demanded* bound meaning. AI is that consumer. The technology the semantic web was early for is, as Part IV shows, exactly what a context data product now assembles — but pulled by a workload that finally makes the authoring effort pay.

There is a fourth attempt the practitioner reader will already be thinking of, and it is a living one rather than a historical one. **Data contracts and data mesh (2019–)** are the nearest current efforts to attach meaning and ownership to data, and this book builds on both rather than replacing them. A data contract binds the *interface promise* — the schema and quality a producer guarantees a consumer; the Data Capsule of Part II extends that idea to bind the *full context* that makes the promise checkable, not just the interface (Chapter 5). Data mesh describes the *operating model* — domain ownership and data-as-a-product — without which neither contracts nor capsules survive contact with an organisation (Chapter 21). Neither has failed; both are young, and both are missing a piece this book supplies. Where the three older attempts stalled, contracts and mesh are the ground the discipline stands on.

The pattern across the three older attempts is instructive and hopeful. Each tried to keep meaning attached, each put the meaning in *a system of its own*, and each therefore inherited the drift it meant to cure — and none had a consumer unforgiving enough to force the discipline through. This book's wager is that binding meaning *to the data* rather than to another system, and doing it now that an unforgiving consumer has arrived to demand it, is what makes the fourth attempt different.

## The inflection: a consumer that cannot supply its own context

We are now in the middle of an AI revolution, and the cliché of the moment is that there is no AI without data engineering. That is true, but it understates the case in a way that matters. The deeper truth is that AI is **the first major consumer of enterprise data that cannot supply its own context.**

An AI model trains on whatever data it is given and learns the patterns in it, including the errors, the contradictions, and the undocumented assumptions. An AI agent answering a question at runtime reads the columns it is given and reasons over them at face value, thousands of times an hour, with no institutional memory and no instinct for when a number smells wrong. The human safeguard — the analyst who filters the noise, who knows that this feed is not to be trusted for that purpose, who notices the duplicate records and quietly works around them — is gone. AI does not work around problems in the data. It learns from them, or it acts on them, and it does so with a confidence that the underlying evidence does not justify.

This is why the unsolved problem of every previous era becomes the binding constraint of this one. For decades we could separate data from its meaning and rely on people to reunite the two on demand. The arrival of a consumer that reads at machine speed, with no judgement and no memory, removes that slack entirely. The gap between data and context, which three generations of architecture widened while human judgement quietly papered over it, is now exposed — and it is exposed at precisely the moment when the stakes, in the form of automated decisions and regulatory scrutiny, are highest.

The numbers are already telling this story, even if the industry is still narrating them as a modelling problem or a talent problem. Surveys find that the majority of AI projects stall, and that when they stall it is overwhelmingly the data underneath them, not the models on top, that is responsible. The next three chapters take that failure apart. But the diagnosis to carry out of this one is simple and it reframes everything that follows: data engineering's history is a triumph of moving data and a quiet, accumulating failure to keep its meaning attached — and AI is the consumer that finally makes that failure unaffordable.

## What this means for the rest of Part I

The instinct, faced with this, is to reach for a technology: a better lake, a better catalogue, a better model. That instinct is exactly the one this part of the book is written to interrupt, because the failure is not in any of those technologies. It is in a mental model — a set of inherited assumptions about how data and its meaning relate — that each era reinforced and none questioned.

So Part I proceeds by taking apart that mental model in three moves. Chapter 2 examines the most consequential of the inherited assumptions: the idea, installed by the data lake, that you should hoard data now and find its meaning later. Chapter 3 traces the specific mechanism by which data and metadata came to live apart, and why every generation re-committed the same separation. And Chapter 4 confronts the failure mode directly — what actually happens when AI meets data that cannot explain itself — and sets out the map that the rest of the book follows to fix it.

The history is not the enemy. The data lake was the right architecture for 2015, the warehouse the right one for 1995. The enemy is the assumption they all shared and never named: that context is something you can keep somewhere else and attach when you need it. For a human consumer, that assumption mostly held. For the consumer that has now arrived, it does not.

## Further reading

- E. F. Codd, *A Relational Model of Data for Large Shared Data Banks* (1970) — the paper that started the structured era.
- W. H. Inmon, *Building the Data Warehouse*; R. Kimball & M. Ross, *The Data Warehouse Toolkit* — the two foundational (and rival) warehouse philosophies.
- J. Dean & S. Ghemawat, *MapReduce: Simplified Data Processing on Large Clusters* (2004), and S. Ghemawat et al., *The Google File System* (2003) — the papers behind Hadoop.
- On the modern-stack "crisis of integration," the RudderStack and Airbyte histories of data engineering are useful practitioner surveys.
- On the semantic web's original ambition, T. Berners-Lee, J. Hendler & O. Lassila, *The Semantic Web* (Scientific American, 2001).

> **Chapter summary.** Data engineering's one enduring job is turning raw data into useful information, and each era — relational/warehouse, Hadoop, cloud/real-time, lakehouse — solved its predecessor's central problem (consistency, scale, speed, fragmentation) while handing forward a new one. The constant, unsolved thread is that data's meaning kept getting separated from the data: modelled in a separate layer, schema in a separate metastore, semantics scattered across the modern stack. Human analysts silently bridged that gap with judgement. AI is the first consumer that cannot — it has the data and only the data — which turns a chronic problem into the binding constraint. The failure is a mental model, not a technology, and the rest of Part I takes it apart.

> **Try this.** Pick the most-used number in your organisation — revenue, active customers, exposure, churn. Ask three people in three different teams to define it precisely and tell you which table it comes from. The variance in their answers is the size of the gap this chapter describes, and it is the gap your AI will fall into first.
