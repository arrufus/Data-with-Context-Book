# Chapter 19 — The Assessment in Practice

> **Where you are:** Part V — *Prove*. Chapters 17 and 18 gave the framework: six surfaces of evidence, five certification dimensions. This chapter is the field. It walks the six surfaces through the people who hit them the hard way — because a framework is abstract until you have watched a green dashboard fail an audit. The six surfaces come in three pairs, and each pair answers one plain question.

---

## Green dashboards, paused models

Three of Maya's colleagues at Meridian ran functions they were rightly proud of, and each was, within the same eighteen months, ambushed by a question their function could not answer. Their stories are the substance of this chapter, because the gap between "we do this well" and "we can prove it for AI" is not visible from inside a well-run programme. It only appears when the awkward question arrives — and by then, the evidence either exists or it does not.

The six evidence surfaces of Chapter 17 organise naturally into three pairs, and it is easiest to assess a data product one pair at a time. **Quality and lineage** answer *can you defend this data?* **Ownership and metadata** answer *is it a real, governed thing?* **Privacy and consumption** answer *do you control how it is used?* We take them in that order, through the people who learned each the expensive way.

## Can you defend this data? Quality and lineage

### The award-winning programme that went silent

Tom runs Meridian's data-quality programme, and by any conventional measure he runs it superbly: twelve thousand tests, two hundred dashboards, quarterly steward reviews across forty domains, a framework benchmarked against three competitors and better than all of them. It won an industry award. Then, on a Wednesday morning, the fraud-detection model was paused for review — the third pause in two months — citing "persistent data anomalies in the feature set." Tom checked the dashboards. All green. Quality score: ninety-four per cent. No incidents, no SLA breaches, no flagged attributes. The steward review from the previous quarter had signed off the same features. The score had hovered between ninety-two and ninety-six for six months.

The dashboards had nothing to tell him about why the model was paused. His framework was not silent because it was broken. It was silent because it was **not the instrument the AI team needed.** This is the structural insight, and it generalises past Tom: a data-quality programme built for one consumer cannot serve a different one. Quality programmes were built for BI reports, executive dashboards, regulatory submissions, the monthly close — periodic, aggregated, human-read signals. AI workloads are continuous, per-record, machine-read. A fraud model evaluates thousands of transactions a second; it cannot screen-scrape a Looker dashboard before each inference.

Four anti-patterns explain why the award-winning programme could not help, and each is a recognition cue worth carrying:

- **The dashboard problem.** Quality status that lives only in a UI is unreachable to a model. If an AI engineer on your team has built their own upstream quality check because the central programme has no API, you have this problem.
- **The aggregate problem.** A single composite score is averaged across attributes, time, and records. An attribute broken in six per cent of records produces six per cent broken predictions, invisible in a 94% average. AI consumes records, not averages. Ask which AI workload could make a decision from your flagship composite score; the answer is none.
- **The cadence problem.** Weekly stewards, monthly committees, quarterly audits are the rhythms of human organisational life. A degradation the quarterly review catches is, by definition, three months of mispredictions before the catch. If your detection-to-notification time is measured in days, your models run on last week's quality state.
- **The contract problem.** A central programme assesses every product against one generic standard, which is by construction the wrong size for every consumer. A fraud model needs sub-second freshness on a few features and is indifferent to the rest; a regulatory return needs verifiable lineage on specific fields.

What good looks like is not mysterious: quality **captured as code** (versioned with the product, reviewed in pull requests), **contractual** (explicit commitments to each consumer's use case), **continuous** (running on every release and, where needed, every inference), and **exposed** (available as an API or event, with dashboards downstream of the programmatic surface, not the source of it). Tom's next move is not to *scale* his programme. It is to *invert who it serves* — from the steward reading a dashboard to the model calling an endpoint. This is exactly the quality dimension of Chapters 10 and 18, seen from the inside of a programme that was doing everything right for the wrong consumer.

### The claim and the receipt

Quality, though, is only half of "can you defend this data?" Nadia, who leads model validation at Meridian, learned the other half in a routine quarterly pricing review. The quality dashboard had been green since spring. Twenty minutes in, an actuary asked about one feature — the claims-history ratio — and the question was narrow and deadly: *where does this feature come from, and what did its quality look like on the day this model trained?* The data-engineering lead could name the source system. The quality lead could pull the current score. Nobody could produce **both at once, joined, for one Tuesday in March.** Someone offered to set up a meeting.

That meeting — booked because a simple question had no retrievable answer — is the entire failure. Because **quality is a claim, and lineage is the receipt**, and AI is the first consumer that demands both at once. Quality answers *is this data good?* Lineage answers *what is this data, and how do you know?* And a quality claim is a claim about a moment and a scope, both of which decay: a 98% completeness score describes a whole table at the instant it was measured, but the model trained on a filtered slice, weeks later, after two more pipeline runs. The score that mattered — the quality of *that slice, on that day* — is the one nobody captured, because the dashboard reports the present and models are built in the past. If you cannot retrieve the quality of your training data *as it stood at training time*, what you have is monitoring. It is useful. It is not evidence.

Lineage has the same trap, and it is worth being precise, because two different things get called lineage. The first is the **diagram** — tables, columns, arrows, drawn for a review and refreshed quarterly if anyone remembers. It describes how data is *supposed* to flow, it has been wrong since the second deployment, and business meaning bleeds out at every hop until `clm_hist_adj` means nothing to anyone who did not write the job. The second is **instance-level traceability** — this prediction, this customer, these input values, from this version of this feature, on this date. Almost nobody has it, and almost every hard question needs it. The customer who wants to know why they were refused is asking about their row. Nadia's actuary was not asking for the architecture; he was asking for a receipt. The test is brutal: *if tracing one number to its source takes longer than the meeting in which the question was asked, you do not have lineage — you have archaeology.*

Neither half works alone. Quality without lineage is a true claim about unspecified data — testimony without exhibits. Lineage without quality is a flawless map of the route and total silence about what travelled it — garbage with a meticulous paper trail. The thing an AI workload can actually trust is the **join**: quality assertions bound to the lineage of specific data, captured at the moment that mattered, travelling with the data rather than living in a tool beside it. That joined, travelling artefact is the Data Capsule of Part II. When it exists, Nadia's meeting never gets booked — the actuary's question is a query, the answer arrives before the coffee, and the review moves on.

## Is it a real, governed thing? Ownership and metadata

The churn model drifted in June, and it took most of July to work out why. A data product feeding three dashboards and, latterly, the model had changed underneath everyone: an upstream team redefined a tenure field that used to reset on contract renewal so that it now carried over — sensible on their side, signed off, documented in their own notes. Nothing broke. The dashboards rendered. The model scored. It just got slowly, quietly worse, until a quarter of its predictions rested on a feature that no longer meant what it meant at training time. When someone finally traced it, the question was simple — *who approved the upstream change knowing the model depended on it?* — and the answer was a chain of *not me*. The original steward had moved teams in a reorg. The upstream team did not know the model existed downstream. The wiki page naming the owner named someone who had left in the spring.

Nobody had owned it. Several people had been *assigned* to it. Those are not the same thing, and the gap between them is the ownership surface. **Ownership is authority, not assignment.** Ask an enterprise who owns a data product and you get a name; ask what that name is *allowed to decide* and the certainty leaks away. Who approves a schema change — the team? Who signs off a contract change — the steward, or do they just keep the wiki tidy? Who grants and revokes consumer access? And who tells an upstream team there is a model three hops downstream that will quietly rot if they redefine a field? In most organisations that last question has no owner, because ownership was only ever assignment: a name went in a field and no authority went with it.

AI makes this worse in a specific way. The defining feature of an AI workload is that it creates consumers nobody registered — a model picks up a feature, an agent starts calling a product, a pipeline forms the owner never approved and never sees. The number of people depending on a product *without the owner's knowledge* goes up, so the cost of changes made by people who cannot see who depends on them goes up with it. Ownership that holds looks dull and specific: the owner recorded where a machine can query it, decision rights explicit (this role approves schema, this role approves contracts, this role grants access), an escalation path ending at a named *role* that survives a reorg rather than a named person who might not, and a history of who held authority and when. The test is the churn model's: *if asking who owns a product produces a meeting rather than a name, it is unowned, whatever the wiki says.*

Ownership pairs with metadata because authority over an *undescribed* object is just confident guessing, and a precise description of an object *nobody answers for* is a perfect description of an orphan. The distinction that matters for metadata is not *can be read* — an AI agent will parse a wiki page faster and more patiently than any human — but *can be trusted at runtime.* Helena, who ran Meridian's £400,000 catalogue programme, learned this in a meeting with the head of the new AI programme, who asked one question her flagship achievement could not answer: *can your catalogue tell my team, programmatically, which features in this product changed in the last twenty-four hours?* It could — on a change log that refreshed nightly. Readable, comprehensive, and a day behind the consumer that needed it. A wiki page and a catalogue entry are readable; what they are not is enforceable, current, or bound to the data. Nothing detects when they drift from what the pipeline actually does, and nothing stops a consumer relying on them after they have gone stale. Metadata a machine can trust is a different object: a contract the data carries with it — schema, meaning, constraints, and crucially the owner — expressed as code the pipeline enforces, so that when the data violates the contract, something fails loudly *now* rather than a model degrading quietly over a quarter. A description nobody keeps true and an owner nobody can name are the same failure wearing two hats. Run the churn story again with both in place: the upstream change meets a contract that flags the downstream dependency before it ships, routed to a named owner with the authority to say *wait*; the reorg does not sever the link because the link was a queryable fact, not a memory; the model never drifts. No July. No archaeology.

## Do you control how it is used? Privacy and consumption

The third pair is where Maya's original disaster lived, so it needs less retelling and more naming. A data product can be defensible, owned, and precisely described and *still* fail the moment you cannot say who is allowed to use it and who actually is. **Privacy** asks whether lawful basis, consent, PII handling, and regional rules are enforced *at the product, at runtime* — not aspired to in a policy document held elsewhere. Maya's seventy-three-features question was a privacy-surface question: the policy lived in the privacy team's SharePoint and the data lived in the pipeline, and nothing bound them, so the question had no answer in the room. **Consumption** asks whether the product's consumers — especially the AI ones — are known, contracted with, and monitored. AI consumers become visible only when they break; a product that cannot name its downstreams cannot manage a breaking change, and cannot evolve. Both surfaces have the same cure as the other four: the rule enforced as code and bound to the data, and the consumers registered through governed, contracted endpoints rather than discovered after the incident.

### A fourth story: the consumption surface

The first two pairs got full stories; the third — privacy and consumption — deserves one too, because it is where the least-anticipated failure lives. At Meridian, an internal analytics team, wanting to prototype quickly, pointed a general-purpose LLM at the `client_holdings` product to "explore." Nobody registered them as a consumer. Nobody reviewed what the model would retrieve or retain. For three weeks, confidential holdings data flowed into a vector store the team stood up on the side, outside the governed retrieval path, with no entitlement filter and no retention control. It surfaced only when a curious answer in the prototype referenced a client the analyst had no business seeing — the entitlement failure that a governed index would have prevented at retrieval time. This is the consumption surface's defining hazard: **AI creates consumers nobody registered**, and an unregistered consumer is an ungoverned one. The fix was the discipline of Chapter 8A and Chapter 11 — the product's consumers are registered and contracted, retrieval runs through the governed, entitlement-filtered path, and an unregistered process pointing at the product is *detected* (the active layer sees the new access pattern) rather than discovered by accident. A product that cannot name its consumers cannot control its use; and in the AI era, the consumer it cannot name is the one that will embarrass it.

### Remediation: what each team actually did next

The chapter has diagnosed brilliantly and prescribed nothing, so here are the three teams' 90-day fix plans, because a diagnosis without a runbook is just a better-informed shrug.

**Tom (quality → evidence).** Invert who the programme serves. Weeks 1–4: expose the existing checks as an API and an event, so a model can gate on quality programmatically. Weeks 5–8: move the top-tier products' quality assertions into the repo as code, versioned with the data (Chapter 9). Weeks 9–12: negotiate contracted, per-consumer quality with the AI teams — sub-second freshness on the fraud features, verifiable lineage on the regulated fields — and retire the single composite score as the flagship. The dashboards stay, downstream of the programmatic surface, for humans.

**Nadia (the training-time join).** Make quality and lineage retrievable *together, for a past date*. Weeks 1–6: adopt training-data capsules (Chapter 7) so every model records its pinned snapshot and the quality that snapshot passed. Weeks 7–12: wire the bitemporal lineage graph (Chapter 12) so "what did this feature look like on the day it trained" is a query. The test: the next actuary question is answered in the room, not in a meeting booked for later.

**The churn team (ownership re-chartering).** Give the product an owner with authority. Weeks 1–2: name a data product manager with decision rights over schema, contract, and access — not a steward who keeps a wiki. Weeks 3–8: register consumers and wire the contract so an upstream change flags the downstream dependency before it ships. Weeks 9–12: encode the tenure definition as bound metadata so a redefinition is a detectable, notified event. The reorg-proofing is that the escalation path now ends at a *role*, not a person who might leave in the spring.

## What the assessment reveals

Line the three pairs up and a single shape appears across all six surfaces. In every case, the organisation was doing real, competent work — Tom's tests, Nadia's validation, the steward's documentation, the privacy team's policy — and in every case the work lived *beside* the data rather than *bound to* it, in a form a human could read on a schedule rather than a machine could query in the moment. The assessment is not a hunt for negligence. It is a hunt for the gap between competent-but-separate context and bound-and-queryable evidence. That gap is exactly what Parts II–IV close, which is why certification is cheap for an organisation that has done the earlier work and a nightmare for one that has not.

But notice what recurs underneath every story: a change nobody with authority approved, a consumer nobody registered, a definition nobody owned. These are not evidence-tooling failures. They are **operating-model** failures — diffuse authority, invisible consumers, ownership that was assignment. You can install every capsule and every certification pipeline in this book and still lose the churn model if no one has the funded authority to say *wait*. Evidence makes the failure *visible*; only an operating model makes someone *answer* for it. That is the subject of the next chapter, and it is the one most organisations are least prepared to confront.

## The assessment instrument

The three questions and six surfaces become a *scoring sheet* you can run on any product — the instrument behind the diagnosis, and the seed of Appendix D. Score each surface 0 (reconstruction only / no answer), 1 (monitoring / answer in a week), 2 (bound evidence / answer in the room):

| Surface | Question | Meridian `client_holdings` — before → after |
|---------|----------|:---:|
| Ownership | A name with authority? | 1 → 2 |
| Metadata | Meaning as code? | 0 → 2 |
| Quality | Contracted to the use? | 1 → 2 |
| Lineage | Traceable, point-in-time? | 0 → 2 |
| Privacy | Lawful basis enforced? | 0 → 2 |
| Consumption | Consumers known? | 1 → 2 |
| **Total (/12)** | | **3 → 12** |

A product scoring in the low single digits — where most start, and where Meridian's holdings product began — is not AI-ready however green its dashboards; a product at or near 12 can testify. The instrument's value is that it makes "AI-ready" a *number you can track over a rollout*, not a feeling, and it maps directly onto the certification dimensions of Chapter 18.

### From the other side of the table

It sharpens the exercise to see it from the reviewer's chair for a moment. What an auditor or model-risk reviewer actually looks for is not a clean dashboard — a green dashboard, they have learned, tells you nothing about the past — but *whether the answer arrives without a meeting being booked.* They ask a narrow, dated question (which feature, on which day, under what basis), and they watch what happens next. A team that produces a query and an answer has, in that moment, demonstrated control; a team that offers to "set up a session to trace it" has demonstrated the opposite, regardless of how good the underlying data is. The tell the reviewer is trained on is latency, because latency is the difference between evidence and reconstruction. Knowing this changes how you prepare: you are not preparing a presentation, you are ensuring the six queries return in the room.

### The six surfaces, in a hospital and a SaaS firm

The surfaces are not finance-specific; they translate. In a **hospital**, *ownership* is the named clinician-governance lead accountable for a clinical dataset; *metadata* is whether "abnormal result" is defined as code with the right reference range; *lineage* is tracing a value in a clinical-decision-support suggestion back to the assay and instrument; *privacy* is consent and special-category-data controls enforced at the point an AI scribe reads the record. In a **SaaS company**, *ownership* is the product-analytics owner accountable for the events feeding a churn model; *quality* is contracted freshness on the activity events the model needs within the hour; *consumption* is knowing which internal teams and which AI features consume the "active account" definition, so a redefinition does not silently break three of them. Same six questions, same three degrees of evidence, different vocabulary — which is the point: the assessment is a general instrument, and the wealth-management specifics are illustration, not limitation.

> **Chapter summary.** The six evidence surfaces come in three pairs. **Quality + lineage** (*can you defend this data?*): Tom's award-winning quality programme went silent because it served the wrong consumer — the fix is quality captured as code, contractual, continuous, exposed; and Nadia's paused review showed that quality is a claim while lineage is the receipt, and only their *join*, captured at training time and travelling with the data, is evidence. **Ownership + metadata** (*is it a real, governed thing?*): the churn-model drift showed ownership is authority, not assignment, and metadata must be enforceable-at-runtime, not merely readable. **Privacy + consumption** (*do you control how it is used?*): rules enforced at the product and consumers registered, not discovered on breakage. In every case competent work lived *beside* the data rather than *bound to* it — and the deepest failures are operating-model failures, the subject of Chapter 20.

> **Try this.** Take one production data product and run the three questions in order: can you defend it (retrieve quality *and* lineage for a specific past date), is it governed (get a *name with authority*, not a team, for who approves changes), do you control its use (produce the lawful basis and the full consumer list). Whichever question produces a meeting instead of an answer is the surface to fix first.
